Research Identity

Experimental physicist developing next-generation nano-optical measurement platforms for quantitative light–matter interaction.

My research is motivated by a simple premise: major advances in nanophotonics require not only new materials, but fundamentally new ways to measure them.

I design experimental architectures that merge nanoscale spatial resolution, ultrafast temporal access, and metrological spectral precision, enabling direct observation of physical processes that remain inaccessible to conventional optical techniques.

My scientific trajectory combines two complementary experimental cultures:

  • Optical frequency-comb metrology, developed during my doctoral work at the Politecnico di Milano and expanded through research at the Max Planck Institute for Quantum Optics.
  • Near-field nano-optics, matured since 2022 at CIC nanoGUNE, where I develop s-SNOM-based methodologies to extract quantitative observables from real-space measurements.

Together, these directions support a broad point of view on surface light–matter interaction, in which instrumentation is conceived not merely as a technical tool, but as a path toward predictive electrodynamics at the nanoscale.

I build advanced optical instruments that allow us to observe how light interacts with materials at extremely small scales and over extremely short times. These tools help reveal processes that are normally invisible and support the development of future photonic technologies.

Intellectual Positioning

When a physical phenomenon cannot be measured directly, the priority is to build the instrument that makes it observable.

My research operates at the interface between measurement science and nanophotonics, with the long-term objective of transforming nano-optics from a largely descriptive discipline into a quantitative and predictive framework.

Pillar I — s-SNOM

s-SNOM is a near-field optical technique that circumvents the diffraction limit by localizing electromagnetic fields at the apex of a nanometric probe. It enables imaging and spectroscopy with spatial resolution determined by the probe rather than the wavelength of light.

Vision

To establish near-field nano-optics as a quantitative experimental science, capable of directly measuring the parameters that govern light–matter interaction at deeply subwavelength scales.

Why this matters

Many emergent properties of quantum and nanostructured materials originate on length scales far below the diffraction limit. s-SNOM enables these measurements. My work focuses on pushing the technique beyond imaging toward parameter extraction — including dispersion, damping, coupling strength, and transient response.

Trajectory

Since joining nanoGUNE in 2022, I have developed near-field approaches aimed at linking real-space observables with electrodynamic models.

IR–THz Nanoimaging

Infrared and terahertz nanoimaging reveals phonon and plasmon polaritons whose wavelengths can be orders of magnitude smaller than free-space radiation. Mapping their propagation provides direct insight into confinement, anisotropy, and dissipation at material interfaces.

Scientific opportunity: Dispersion engineering and controlled surface-wave transport.

Nanospectroscopy

Near-field spectroscopy correlates chemical composition, electronic structure, and local optical response within nanometric volumes. Phase-resolved implementations enable quantitative comparison with theory.

Scientific opportunity: Bridging microscopy and precision spectroscopy.

Ultrafast Nanospectroscopy

Integrating femtosecond pump–probe excitation with near-field detection enables access to non-equilibrium dynamics with simultaneous spatial and temporal resolution.

Scientific opportunity: Revealing energy transfer pathways that govern active and hybrid photonic systems.

s-SNOM is an advanced microscopy technique that concentrates light into extremely small regions using a sharp probe, allowing scientists to observe optical behavior far below the resolution of conventional microscopes.

What it is

Near-field microscopy uses a nanoscale probe to concentrate light into regions much smaller than its wavelength, allowing us to see optical behavior at the scale where many material properties originate.

Why it matters

Modern materials often behave differently at the nanoscale. Understanding these local responses is essential for designing faster electronics, improved sensors, and next-generation optical devices.

IR–THz Nanoimaging

This technique lets us visualize how electromagnetic waves travel along a material surface, like watching ripples move across water but at a scale thousands of times smaller than a human hair.

Impact: Seeing these waves directly helps design materials that guide light more efficiently.

Nanospectroscopy

Nanospectroscopy identifies the chemical and electronic properties of materials at extremely small scales.

Impact: It connects composition with function, helping researchers understand why materials behave the way they do.

Ultrafast Nanospectroscopy

By combining ultrafast laser pulses with near-field microscopy, we can observe how materials respond immediately after being excited — essentially filming processes that occur in trillionths of a second.

Impact: Understanding these rapid processes is key to developing faster and more efficient technologies.

Pillar II — Optical Frequency Combs

Optical frequency combs are laser sources whose spectra consist of evenly spaced, phase-coherent lines that function as precise rulers in frequency space. They provide a direct link between optical measurements and primary standards, forming the backbone of modern precision spectroscopy.

Vision

To transfer the rigor of precision metrology into complex optical measurement platforms, enabling experiments that are not only sensitive but traceable and reproducible.

Why this matters

Frequency combs transform spectroscopy from a relative measurement into an absolute one, connecting optical observations directly to primary standards.

Trajectory

My work with frequency combs began during my PhD at Politecnico di Milano and expanded at the Max Planck Institute for Quantum Optics, where I contributed to dual-comb methodologies for precision spectroscopy and holography.

Frequency Metrology

Frequency combs provide absolute calibration across broad bandwidths, ensuring reproducibility between experiments and laboratories.

Scientific opportunity: Establishing reliable material parameters for precision spectroscopy.

Dual-Comb Spectroscopy

Dual-comb spectroscopy replaces mechanical scanning with optical sampling, enabling rapid broadband acquisition while preserving high spectral resolution.

Scientific opportunity: Quantitative spectroscopy compatible with dynamic systems.

Dual-Comb Holography

Dual-comb holography extends metrological precision to coherent imaging, enabling scan-free wavefront reconstruction and hyperspectral three-dimensional measurements.

Scientific opportunity: Linking spectral accuracy with spatial phase information.

Optical frequency combs are specialized lasers that produce perfectly ordered colors of light. This structure allows scientists to measure optical frequencies with extraordinary accuracy.

What they are

Frequency combs are laser sources whose light forms a perfectly ordered set of colors. This structure makes them ideal tools for extremely precise measurements.

Why they matter

They allow scientists to measure light with exceptional accuracy, improving everything from spectroscopy to imaging.

Frequency Metrology

Frequency combs act as precise rulers for light.

Impact: Accurate measurements are the foundation of reliable science and technology.

Dual-Comb Spectroscopy

This method records detailed spectra very quickly without moving mechanical parts.

Impact: Faster measurements allow researchers to study systems that change over time.

Dual-Comb Holography

This technique combines precise spectroscopy with three-dimensional imaging.

Impact: It opens the door to highly accurate optical measurements that capture both structure and composition.

Key Scientific Questions

  • Which observables fundamentally govern polariton transport and dissipation?
  • How can nanoscale measurements achieve metrological reliability?
  • What experimental conditions enable controllable strong and ultrastrong coupling?
  • How can instrumentation reshape the questions we are able to ask?

Experimental Philosophy

I view instrumentation as a scientific contribution in its own right. By designing measurement platforms that unify spatial resolution, temporal access, and spectral accuracy, my objective is to transform nano-optics into a predictive discipline capable of guiding the next generation of photonic technologies.
